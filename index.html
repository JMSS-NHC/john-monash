John Monash:
The Australian Icon

John Monash is one of the most recognisable contemporary icons. He served Australia as a soldier, an engineer and a commissioner. Despite this status, his actions and ideas are not often discussed nor widely known outside of his military career.

This website aims to explore the ways that Monash has influenced Australia through four perspectives, at four points of his life, and in four key turning points in Australian history: The erosion of English identity in Australia; Australia's involvement in the Great War; The construction of the Victorian grid; and the then largest funeral procession in Australian history. The interactive timeline below briefly covers the context in which these four events took place. Click on the four major turning points to learn more about these aspects of his life.

TIMELINE

27th of June, 1865 - John Monash is born to Polish-German Australians Louis Monash and Bertha nee Manasse. He is born in West Melbourne and is the only son of his Jewish parents. Click here to read more about his heritage.
February 1915 - John Monash trains ANZAC soldiers in Cairo
19th December 1915 - The battle of Gallipoli proves to be too arduous. Monash and his men successfully evacuate the grounds.
4th July 1918 - John Monash leads the battle of Hamel, resulting in a decisive win for the allies through his innovative tactics. (Click here to find out more)
8th August 1918 - John Monash leads the battle of Amiens, gaining 21km of land for the allies.
12th August 1918 - King George V knights John Monash for his efforts in the great war.
1918 - Monash is tasked with infiltrating German coal mines to report on their brown coal processing practices.
late 1920 - Victoria is in growing demand for electric power. Monash is appointed chairman of the State Electricity Commission, which seeks to meet these demands where businesses are not. The commission's solution is to launch the largest coordinated state infrastructure program at the time, and Monash is tasked with spearheading the movement. (Click here to find out More)
1931 - Sir John Monash dies. His funeral is the largest in Australia with over 300 000 attendees.

How research was conducted

In approaching our topic, we delved into the birth of John Monash, his heritage, contributions as a soldier in WW1 and eventually leading to his life as a civil engineer and concluding with his funeral and legacies left behind. Older articles detailing critical events related to John Monash was available using NLA’s Trove service and the Parliament of Victoria's Public Records. The NAA provided a resource to explore different items related to John Monash. Access to multiple books allowed for wider reading of John Monash and a deeper understanding of the period of history he occupied. The overall collation of information allowed us to determine John Monash’s impact to being a turning point factor in many events and decisions.
